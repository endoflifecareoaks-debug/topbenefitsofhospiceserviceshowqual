What is Hospice?

Hospice is specialized compassionate care for individuals facing a life-limiting illness. It focuses on comfort rather than cure, offering pain relief and symptom management. Hospice supports patients emotionally, physically, and spiritually during their final stage of life. Families also receive counseling, guidance, and respite support. The goal of hospice is to ensure dignity, peace, and improved quality of life.

FAQ's

Hospice (or end-of-life care) is a type of care focused on comfort, dignity and quality of life — for individuals with a life-limiting illness who choose to focus on comfort rather than curative treatment. It provides medical, emotional, and spiritual support for patients and their families.

Typically, hospice is available to people whose medical condition is terminal or life-limiting, and whose healthcare team — often including a physician and medical director — determines that curative treatment is no longer effective or desired.

Hospice care can be offered in a patient's home, in a nursing home, assisted living facility, or a dedicated hospice facility — wherever the patient feels most comfortable. The hospice team works in coordination with physicians, caregivers, or family members to deliver personalized, compassionate care.
